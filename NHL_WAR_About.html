What is WAR?

Wins Above Replacement — the core metric

WAR (Wins Above Replacement) measures how many wins a player adds to their team compared to a freely available replacement-level player — someone you could call up from the minors or sign off the waiver wire. It converts all on-ice production into a single number on the wins scale.
The core conversion: In hockey, roughly 6 goals separate a playoff team from a replacement-level team over a full season. So throughout this model, 1 WAR = 6 Goals Above Replacement (GAR).
A player with +3.0 WAR is estimated to have contributed 3 more wins than a replacement player in the same role and ice time. The league average full-time NHLer typically sits around +1–2 WAR; elite players reach +6–10; replacement level is near 0.
Note on data: All underlying stats (xGoals, ice time, Corsi, etc.) come from Natural Stat Trick. This model uses the 2025-26 season CSV exports.

WAR Components

Total WAR = EV Offense + EV Defense + PP + PK + Finishing

EV Offense WAR 5-on-5
Measures a player's individual offensive contribution at even strength relative to a replacement-level player. It combines expected goals with playmaking (weighted assists) to capture both shooting and setup.
iOff60 = (xGoals + 0.75 × PrimaryAssists + 0.5 × SecondaryAssists) / icetime_hours × 60 -- Individual offensive rate per 60 minutes at 5-on-5 EV_Off_GAR = (iOff60 − replacement_iOff60) × icetime_hours EV_Off_WAR = EV_Off_GAR ÷ 6 -- replacement_iOff60 = 15th percentile among qualified players of same position group -- Forwards and Defensemen have separate replacement levels -- Minimum 300 seconds of 5-on-5 ice time to qualify for replacement level calculation
Why 0.75 and 0.5 for assists? Primary assists are more skill-driven (direct setup of a shot) while secondary assists carry more luck. The weights reflect their relative predictive value for future performance.
EV Defense WAR 5-on-5
Measures a player's defensive impact by comparing how their team suppresses expected goals when they're on the ice vs. when they're off the ice. A player who is on the ice for fewer xGoals against than their teammates gives up in their absence gets a positive score.
onIce_xGA_share = OnIce_xGoalsAgainst / (OnIce_xGoalsFor + OnIce_xGoalsAgainst) offIce_xGA_share = OffIce_xGoalsAgainst / (OffIce_xGoalsFor + OffIce_xGoalsAgainst) def_relative = offIce_xGA_share − onIce_xGA_share -- Positive = suppressed more xGA than teammates did without them EV_Def_GAR = def_relative × xGoals_context_rate × icetime_hours EV_Def_WAR = EV_Def_GAR ÷ 6
Why on-ice vs. off-ice? This approach partially controls for team quality — if a player is on a weak defensive team, their off-ice numbers will also look bad, so the differential isolates their personal contribution rather than just reflecting their teammates.
Power Play WAR 5-on-4
Measures individual offensive contribution on the power play, compared to a replacement-level power play player. The replacement level here is set at 40% of the league average PP production rate — reflecting that a replacement player on the PP would produce at a well-below-average rate.
pp_iOff60 = (PP_xGoals + 0.75 × PP_PrimaryAssists + 0.5 × PP_SecondaryAssists) / pp_hours × 60 repl_pp60 = weighted_average(all pp_iOff60) × 0.40 -- Weighted by ice time, among players with >60 seconds PP icetime PP_GAR = (pp_iOff60 − repl_pp60) × pp_hours PP_WAR = PP_GAR ÷ 6
Penalty Kill WAR 4-on-5
Measures how well a player suppresses expected goals against while shorthanded, relative to the league average PK xGA rate. Positive PK WAR means the player kept their team closer to the league average xGA rate (or better) while killing penalties.
pk_xGA60 = OnIce_xGoalsAgainst / pk_hours -- Player's on-ice xGA rate per 60 min while shorthanded avg_pk_xGA60 = weighted_average(all pk_xGA60) -- Weighted by ice time, among players with >60 seconds PK icetime PK_GAR = (avg_pk_xGA60 − pk_xGA60) × pk_hours -- Positive = gave up less xGA than the average PK player PK_WAR = PK_GAR ÷ 6
Finishing WAR All Situations
The difference between a player's actual goals and their expected goals. Positive Finishing means the player scored more than the model predicted — either due to shooting talent, luck, or a combination. This is the most volatile component year-to-year.
Finishing = Goals − xGoals (all situations) Finishing_WAR = Finishing ÷ 6
Interpret with caution: Finishing is noisy. A player with +5 Finishing in one season will often regress toward zero the next. It's included in Total WAR for completeness, but EV Offense + Defense tell a more stable story.
Goalie WAR GSAE ÷ 6
Goalie WAR is built on GSAE (Goals Saved Above Expected) — the difference between the goals a goalie was expected to allow and the goals they actually allowed. A positive GSAE means the goalie outperformed the model; negative means they underperformed.
GSAE = xGoals_faced − Goals_allowed -- xGoals_faced: what an average goalie would allow given the shots faced -- Positive = goalie saved more than expected Goalie_WAR = GSAE ÷ 6 Save% = (Shots_on_goal − Goals_allowed) / Shots_on_goal xSave% = (Shots_on_goal − xGoals_faced) / Shots_on_goal Sv% − xSv% = how much better/worse than expected
HD GSAE (High Danger Goals Saved Above Expected) applies the same logic but only to high-danger shots — typically the most telling indicator of goalie skill since these are the hardest shots to save and require active intervention rather than just positioning.
WAR / 82 Pace stat
Projects a player's WAR to a full 82-game season, accounting for games missed due to injury, healthy scratches, or call-ups. Useful for comparing players with different games played — a player with 2.0 WAR in 41 games is on pace for the same full-season production as someone with 4.0 WAR in 82 games.
WAR_per82 = Total_WAR / games_played × 82

Percentile Ranks

How player cards show relative performance

Each WAR bar on a player card shows a percentile rank among their position group peers — forwards are ranked against other forwards, defensemen against defensemen, goalies against goalies. The percentile shows what proportion of peers the player outperforms.
percentile = count(peers with lower value) / (total peers − 1) × 100 -- A 90th percentile EV Offense player outperforms 90% of peers at their position
Percentiles are calculated across all players in the dataset regardless of games played filter — so the filter on the leaderboard changes who's displayed, but not their underlying percentile rank.

Replacement Level

The baseline every player is measured against

A "replacement player" represents the freely available talent pool — someone who can be called up from the AHL, claimed off waivers, or signed as a depth signing. Players at or below replacement level provide negative value relative to what's easily available.
EV replacement level = 15th percentile of iOff60 among qualified skaters -- Calculated separately for Forwards and Defensemen -- Qualified = at least 300 seconds (5 minutes) of 5-on-5 ice time PP replacement level = league average PP production rate × 40% PK replacement level = league average PK xGA allowed rate
The 15th percentile cutoff is a common convention in hockey analytics, roughly reflecting the production level of the marginal NHL roster player. This means approximately 85% of qualified skaters provide positive EV Offense value.

Possession & Shot Quality Stats

The building blocks used across all three pages

xGoals Expected Goals
A probability-based measure of shot quality. Each shot is assigned a likelihood of becoming a goal based on factors like shot location, shot type, and whether it followed a rebound. xGoals models out luck — a player who generates high-xG shots is doing the right things even if the puck isn't going in yet.
xGF% Expected Goals For %
The share of total expected goals that belonged to a player or team's side. Above 50% means the player/team is generating more quality chances than they're giving up.
xGF% = xGoalsFor / (xGoalsFor + xGoalsAgainst)
CF% Corsi For %
Share of total shot attempts (on goal + missed + blocked) belonging to a player or team. A pure possession metric — if a team has 60% CF%, they're directing 60% of all shot attempts. Corsi is less predictive than xGF% but is a useful volume proxy.
CF% = ShotAttemptsFor / (ShotAttemptsFor + ShotAttemptsAgainst)
FF% Fenwick For %
Like Corsi but excludes blocked shots. Unblocked shot attempts are generally considered a better proxy for puck possession than total attempts since blocking shots is a defensive skill that can inflate Corsi for weaker teams.
FF% = UnblockedShotAttemptsFor / (UnblockedFor + UnblockedAgainst)
HDxGF% High Danger
The xGF% calculation applied only to high-danger shot locations — typically the slot and crease area directly in front of the net. These shots have a much higher base conversion rate and are harder to generate, making this metric a quality signal on top of a quantity signal.

Lines & Pairings Page

What each column shows

Lines data is 5-on-5 only. Each row represents a unique forward line or defensive pairing — any combination of players who skated together at even strength. Combos with more ice time are more meaningful; the default minimum TOI filter (100 min) removes small samples.
ColumnWhat it means
TOI (min)Total 5-on-5 ice time together in minutes. Higher = more data, more reliable.
xGF%Expected goals share while this combination was on the ice. The primary quality measure.
CF%Shot attempt share — possession volume for this combination.
GF / GAGoals for and against while this line/pairing was on the ice together.
xGF / xGAExpected goals for and against — smooths out shooting/goalie luck.
HDxGF%High danger expected goals share — are they dominating the slot battle?
HDxGF / HDxGAHigh danger expected goals raw totals for each side.
Small sample caveat: Even a 100-minute filter only covers roughly 1–2 games' worth of even-strength ice time. Lines with under 300–500 minutes together should be read as directional signals rather than firm conclusions.
